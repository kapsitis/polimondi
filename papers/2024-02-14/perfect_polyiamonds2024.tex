\begin{abstract}
This presentation introduces Polyiamonds, Magic and Perfect Polyiamonds
as well as some narrower classes of perfect polyiamonds, and 
provides inductive definitions of infinite families of perfect polyiamonds
to establish the necessary and sufficient conditions for their existence.
The inductive definitions are expressed in the form of formal grammars. 

Several formalisms are considered, including Regular Grammars,
Context-Free Grammars (CFGs) and TAG (Tree-Adjoining Grammars), 
and Parallel Context-Free Grammars (PCLs).
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

Here are definitions from the theory of formal languages. 

\begin{definition}
A {\em language} is a set of strings over a finite alphabet.
A language is {\em regular} if it can be generated by a regular grammar,
and {\em context-free} if it can be generated by a context-free grammar. 
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
A {\em Tree Adjoining Grammar (TAG)}\cite{Joshi1997} is a tuple $G = (N, \Sigma, I, A, S)$ where $N$ is a finite set of nonterminal symbols, $\Sigma$ is a finite set of terminal symbols, $I$ is a finite set of initial trees, $A$ is a finite set of auxiliary trees, and $S \in N$ is the start symbol.
The language generated by $G$ is the set of all strings that can be derived from the
initial trees by applying the operations of substitution and adjunction using 
the auxiliary trees.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
A {\em parallel context-free language (PCL)}\cite{Siromoney1974} is a 
language generated by a parallel context-free grammar, which is a tuple 
$G = (N, \Sigma, P, S)$ where $N$ is a finite set of nonterminal symbols, 
$\Sigma$ is a finite set of terminal symbols, $P$ is a finite set of 
production rules, and $S \in N$ is the start symbol.
\end{definition}







\begin{definition}
A {\em polyiamond} is a simple polygon consisting of 
identical equilateral triangles.
A {\em magic $n$-polyiamond} is a polyiamond with $n$ sides of lengths
$1,2,\ldots,n$ in some order.
\end{definition}


\begin{definition}
A {\em perfect $n$-polyiamond}\cite{APSITIS1} is a polyiamond whose 
sides are of lengths $n,n-1,\ldots,1$ in this order. 
(Thus, a perfect $n$-polyiamond is a magic $n$-polyiamond, but not
vice versa.)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
A {\em perfect acute polyiamond} has all interior angles 
$60^{\circ}$ or $300^{\circ}$, a {\em perfect obtuse polyiamond} 
has all angles $120^{\circ}$ or $240^{\circ}$.
\end{definition}

Perfect polyiamonds can be represented as strings over the alphabet 
$\Sigma = \{ \mathtt{a}, \mathtt{b}, \mathtt{c}, \mathtt{d}, \mathtt{e}, \mathtt{f} \}$ 
listing their side directions in decreasing length order.
Specifying the lengths is not necessary, as they are determined 
by the position in the string.
The set of all perfect polyiamonds can be viewed as a language over $\Sigma$: 

$$\mathcal{P} = \{ w \in \Sigma^* \mid w \text{ encodes a perfect polyiamond} \}.$$

To avoid symmetric cases (rotations and reflections),
we typically assume that the longest side for an encoded perfect polyiamond 
is in direction $\mathtt{a}$, and the second longest side is 
either in direction $\mathtt{b}$ or $\mathtt{c}$, so that the first 
two symbols of the string are $\mathtt{a}\mathtt{b}$ or $\mathtt{a}\mathtt{c}$.


\begin{definition}
The set $\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{P}$ of all perfect acute polyiamonds 
with internal angles $60^\circ$ or $300^\circ$.
The set $\mathcal{O} \subseteq \mathcal{P}$ of all perfect obtuse polyiamonds 
with internal angles $120^\circ$ or $240^\circ$.
\end{definition}

\section{Results}

{\bf The necessary conditions for the existence of perfect acute polyiamonds:}

\begin{proposition}
A perfect acute n-polyiamond can only exist if
$n \equiv 3 \pmod{6}$ or $n \equiv 5 \pmod{6}$.
\end{proposition}

\textbf{Proof:}

\noindent (1) Side directions are ``\textbf{a}'', ``\textbf{c}'' or ``\textbf{e}''.
The sides $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ are split into 3
disjoint subsets of equal sums.
Hence $\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$ is divisible by 3.

\medskip

\noindent (2) For even $n = 2k$ the sum of
internal angles is $(2k - 2) \cdot 180^\circ$.
Let $m$ of these angles be $60^\circ$. Then

\begin{align*}
(2k - 2) \cdot 180^\circ &= m \cdot 60^\circ + (2k - m) \cdot 300^\circ \\
6k - 6 &= m + (2k - m) \cdot 5 \\
4m &= 4k + 6 \\
0 &\equiv 2 \pmod{4}
\end{align*}

A contradiction. \qedsymbol





\begin{proposition}
A perfect obtuse $n$-polyiamond can only exist if
\[
n \equiv 0 \pmod{6}
\]
\end{proposition}

\textbf{Proof:} 
For odd $n = 2k + 1$ the sum of inner angles:

\begin{align*}
180^\circ(2k - 1) &= 120^\circ m + 240^\circ(2k - 1 - m) \\
3(2k - 1) &= 2m + 4(2k - 1 - m) \\
1 &\equiv 0 \pmod{2}
\end{align*}

Contradiction. So $n$ must be even.

\medskip

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=2.5in]{perfect_polyiamonds2024_red_blue_arrows.png}
\end{center}

For red direction vectors $(\mathtt{a},\mathtt{c},\mathtt{e})$ we have
$x - y \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$.

For blue direction vectors $(\mathtt{b},\mathtt{d},\mathtt{f})$ we have
$x - y \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$. 

In an obtuse polyiamond they alternate -- all red
vectors are even; blue vectors are odd.
Adding any side of a perfect obtuse polyiamond
increases the difference $(x - y)$ by $2 \pmod{3}$.
So the number of sides $\boldsymbol{n}$ is divisible by 3. \qedsymbol




\section{Linear Analysis of Perfect Polygonal Chains}

Assume that we need to add vectors for some fragment within a perfect polyiamond, 
e.g. $\mathtt{a}\mathtt{b}\mathtt{c}\mathtt{d}\mathtt{e}\mathtt{f}$. 
As usual, the last side in direction $\mathtt{f}$ is the shortest, e.g. of length $|z|$, 
where $|z|$ is the suffix following the fragment $\mathtt{abcdef}$.  
Side $\mathtt{a}$ is the longest ($|z| + 5$). 

We decompose it as a sum of two vectors: one of them is the sum of unit
vecors in all these directions multiplied by $|z|$, and the other is 
a "staircase" sum of unit vectors in these directions multiplied by $6,5,4,3,2,1$ respectively.

\[
\begin{array}{rcl}
\mathbb{g}(\mathtt{abcdef}) & = & 6(1,0,-1) + 5(1,-1,0) + 4(0,-1,1) + 3(-1,0,1) + 2(-1,1,0) + (0,1,-1) 
= (6,-6,0)\\
\mathbb{p}(\mathtt{abcdef}) & = & (1,0,-1) + (1,-1,0) + (0,-1,1) + (-1,0,1) + (-1,1,0) + (0,1,-1) = (0,0,0)\\
\end{array}
\]

\includegraphics[width=4in]{perfect_polyiamonds2024_stair_functions.png}


\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:pumping}
Let $x,y,z \in \{\mathtt{a},\mathtt{b},\mathtt{c},\mathtt{d},\mathtt{e},\mathtt{f}\}^{*}$
be three sequences of direction letters and $|y| \neq 0$.
Define $w_n = x y^n z$ for any positive $n$.
Then some $w_n$ are not perfect polyiamonds.
\end{proposition}

{\bf Proof:} 
Sums of side vectors:

\[
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
w_1 &= g(z) + g(y) + g(x) + |z|\,p(y) + (|y| + |z|)\,p(x) = (0,0,0),\\
w_2 &= g(z) + 2g(y) + g(x) + (2|z| + |y|)\,p(y) + (2|y| + |z|)\,p(x) = (0,0,0),\\
w_3 &= g(z) + 3g(y) + g(x) + (3|z| + 3|y|)\,p(y) + (3|y| + |z|)\,p(x) = (0,0,0).
\end{aligned}
\right.
\]


After simplification we get $|y|p(y) = (0,0,0)$ and therefore
$\mathbb{p}(y) = (0,0,0)$.

If $\mathbb{p}(y) \neq (0,0,0)$, then repeating $y$ moves the chain in a nonzero
direction. The displacement contributed by the pumped block grows linearly with $n$,
whereas the suffix $z$ is fixed and has finite total length. Hence for sufficiently
large $n$ the chain cannot return to its starting point, so $w_n$ is not even a
closed polygonal chain and therefore is not a perfect polyiamond.

If $\mathbb{p}(y) = (0,0,0)$, then the claim follows from Lemma~\ref{lemma:spiralling} below:
for sufficiently large $n$, the repeated block $y^n$ either self-intersects or traps
the remaining finite suffix inside a contracting spiral, so $w_n$ is not a
simple closed polygon. Therefore some $w_n$ are not perfect polyiamonds.


\begin{lemma}
\label{lemma:spiralling}
If $p(y) = (0,0,0)$, then appending multiple copies of such $y$ 
we get a sequence of polygonal chains:
$y^1, y^2, y^3, \ldots$
with decreasing side lengths.
For any sequence $y$ exactly one statement is true:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The polygonal chain will intersect itself
    (its $2$nd iteration intersects its $1$st iteration).
    \item The sequence $y^1, y^2, y^3, \ldots$ creates a contracting 
    spiral where the corresponding sides in adjacent iterations are parallel; 
    and the distances between these parallel sides have a constant upper bound.
\end{itemize}
\end{lemma}

{\bf Proof (sketch):}
Since $p(y)=(0,0,0)$, each copy of $y$ has zero net unit-vector sum; thus each
iteration starts where the previous one starts, but with uniformly shorter sides.
Corresponding sides in consecutive iterations are parallel and their offsets are
uniformly bounded by a constant depending only on $y$.

If two consecutive iterations intersect, we are in the first case.
If they do not intersect, the nesting of successive iterations forces a contracting
spiral structure with bounded gaps between corresponding sides, yielding the second
case. \qedsymbol

\begin{corollary}
There is no infinite set of perfect polyiamonds that is a 
regular language over $\Sigma$. 
\end{corollary}

\textbf{Proof:}
Assume, for contradiction, that an infinite regular language
$L\subseteq\Sigma^{*}$ contains only perfect polyiamonds.
By the Pumping Lemma, every sufficiently long $w\in L$ can be written
$w=xyz$ with $|y|>0$ such that $xy^nz\in L$ for all $n\ge 0$.

By Proposition~\ref{prop:pumping}, for any such decomposition there exists some $n$ for which
$xy^nz$ is not a perfect polyiamond:
either $p(y)\neq (0,0,0)$ and the chain cannot close for large $n$, or
$p(y)=(0,0,0)$ and by Lemma~3.4 large enough pumping creates
self-intersection or a trapping contracting spiral.
This contradicts $xy^nz\in L$ for all $n$. Therefore no such infinite regular
language exists.  
$\blacksquare$

\section{Conclusions}

\begin{itemize}
\item Perfect polyiamonds are a rich source of examples and counterexamples 
in the theory of formal languages.
\item The set of perfect polyiamonds is not a regular language, 
but it contains infinite subsets that are context-free languages.
\item Many infinite sequences of perfect polyiamonds can be generated by a parallel context-free 
grammar, which allows for more complex structures than context-free grammars. 
\item The study of perfect polyiamonds can lead to insights into the limitations 
of various types of grammars and the properties of formal languages.
\item The set of perfect polyiamonds can be extended to include perfect closed polygonal 
chains, which may have intersecting sides, providing a broader class of objects to study.
\end{itemize}
